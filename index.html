Adenoviruses in Gene Therapy and Vaccine Development

Adenoviruses are double stranded DNA non-enveloped viruses that are contained in an icosahedral capsid studded with fiber proteins on the vertices. The adenovirus particle is 70-90 nanometers in size, so it is far smaller than the average cell which is about 100,000 nanometers in diameter; yet it is still considered to be a medium sized virus. Adenoviruses were discovered in 1953 and there are more than 50 human serotypes that most commonly cause illness of the respiratory system, ie. the common cold.

Adenoviruses are easy to grow in the laboratory, maintain a high level of infectivity and are easy to genetically manipulate by inserting genes of interest into the viruses' double stranded DNA. Because of their defined replication cycle and features mentioned above, adenoviruses are often chosen to be the workhorses for gene therapy and vaccine development.

Gene therapy is a technique in which the gene of a patient is modified or replaced so that a disease-state is modified or eliminated. Gene therapy requires some vehicle to carry and insert the gene of choice that will change or replace the diseased gene into the patient's genome. Generally, this requires infecting a subset of cells in the body. To make the vectors for gene therapy, the viral genes of adenoviruses (who's genome typically containing 23-46 protein coding viral genes) are removed and the genes of interest to be expressed in the patient are introduced. To some extent this procedure limits the amount and size of genes of interest that are integrated into the modified adenoviral genome because the viral capsid's structure creates a hard limit on how much double stranded DNA can be introduced. Because many clinical trials have found that adenoviral vectors are safe and well tolerated in humans, adenoviral vectors have also been used in trials for the treatment of cancers.

In vaccine development, it is important that the vaccine is safe, generates an antibody response, and it is also advantageous for the vaccine to elicit a T cell response in the patient. Antibodies can neutralize pathogens or alert the immune system to destroy a particular target. The next objective is to drive a strong T cell response to support antibody production or stimulate cytotoxic T cells to kill any pathogens that have evaded the antibodies. Adenoviral vectors have been chosen for vaccine development because it is easy to genetically engineer with genes for a particular virus threat. Also, a single replication competent adenovirus can create as many as 10,000 copies of itself from one viral particle. This amplifies the inserted viral genes that are important for creating the antibody and T cell responses. The downside of using adenoviral vectors in humans is that in immunocompromised patients, they may display more dangerous side effects from the adenovirus. This has been recently addressed though in the development of single-cycle adenovirus vectors. What this means is that normally an adenoviral vector not only makes 10,000 copies of it's genome, it also makes 10,000 viral particles that can infect other cells in the body. Single-cycle adenovirus vectors do not have that property so they only replicate the genes of interest (for example for the viral vaccine) in the infected cell.

In conclusion, adenoviruses have been very useful for biological production because of their genetic tractability of their double stranded DNA genome and the evidence that most adenoviral vectors are safe and well tolerated in human patients. Their utility as drivers for foreign gene expression is highlighted in their uses in gene therapy and in vaccine development.

Glossary

Peptide

A small protein fragment consisting of a chain of amino acids

Retroviruses

An RNA virus which generates a double stranded DNA copy in the host cell by reverse transcription. Subsequently the viral double stranded DNA is integrated into the host genome.

Viroids

A single stradned circular RNA that is the smallest pathgenic agent discovered. Note that a viroid lacks a viral capsid. Infects plants.
